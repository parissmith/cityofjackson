Executive Summary

Jackson's economy is anchored by government, healthcare, education, legal/professional services, nonprofit services, hospitality, transportation, and industrial activity. The business data shows a strong daytime employment base compared with the residential population, meaning Jackson functions as a regional job center.

Key takeaway: Jackson is not just a residential market; it is Mississippi's capital-city employment center. The growth opportunity is to better connect corridors, vacant/underused parcels, small businesses, and workforce development.

The parcel data should be used to identify where business activity is already concentrated and where low-value or vacant-like parcels could support redevelopment, small business expansion, infill, mixed-use activity, or corridor beautification.

Overall Workforce Analysis

Government and public administration appear to be the dominant employment base, supported by federal, state, county, and city offices. This creates stable employment and reinforces Jackson's role as the state capital.

Healthcare and social assistance are also major job categories, with clinics, health plans, community health providers, home health services, and social-service organizations represented in the report.

Professional services are one of Jackson's strongest private-sector clusters. Legal, engineering, architecture, accounting, consulting, insurance, and finance firms create higher-wage office jobs, especially downtown and near Capitol Street.

Education, nonprofit, and community development jobs are highly visible. These sectors support residents directly and create opportunities for workforce training, neighborhood revitalization, and grant-funded implementation.

Industrial, transportation, hospitality, and retail jobs are present but more corridor-specific. These sectors can be strengthened through corridor redevelopment, small business support, site readiness, and better use of vacant parcels.

Strategic workforce message: Jackson offers a mix of stable public-sector jobs, healthcare/service jobs, high-skill professional jobs, and mission-driven nonprofit jobs. The biggest growth gap is expanding technology, entrepreneurship, advanced manufacturing, logistics, and neighborhood-scale commercial jobs.
